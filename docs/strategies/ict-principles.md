# The ICT Framework: A Principled Guide to Reading Price

**By the principles of the Inner Circle Trader methodology**

---

## Preface

Everything you need to understand about price is already on the chart. The problem is that retail traders have been taught to read charts through the lens of indicators, pattern recognition, and probability models that have nothing to do with how price actually moves. Price does not move because of supply and demand zones, it does not move because RSI hit 70, and it certainly does not move because a head-and-shoulders pattern "completed."

Price moves because of one thing: **liquidity**. Specifically, it moves because smart money — institutional order flow — needs liquidity to fill positions. Every concept in this guide flows from that single truth. If you understand liquidity, you understand price. If you do not, no amount of indicator stacking will save you.

This is not a shortcut. This is a framework that demands you study charts, mark up price action by hand, and develop the discipline to wait for what the market gives you. There is no holy grail here — only principles that, when understood deeply, give you an edge that no retail indicator ever will.

## Part I: The Foundation — How Markets Actually Work

### Liquidity: The Engine of All Price Movement

Liquidity, in the context we use it, refers to resting orders in the market. When a retail trader places a stop loss, that stop loss is a resting order. When thousands of retail traders all place stop losses at the same obvious level — below a swing low, above a swing high — that cluster of orders becomes a **liquidity pool**.

Institutions cannot simply enter the market the way you do. When you buy one contract of ES, the market does not move. When an institution needs to deploy a position worth hundreds of millions of dollars, they need someone on the other side of that trade. They need liquidity. They need your stop losses.

This is why price repeatedly sweeps obvious highs and lows before reversing. It is not manipulation in the conspiratorial sense — it is the mechanical reality of how large orders get filled. The market is a transfer mechanism, and understanding who is transferring what to whom is the entire game.

**How it shows up on the chart:**

Look at any swing low that has been tested multiple times. Retail sees "support." What is actually there is a pool of buy stops (stop losses from shorts) and sell stops (stop losses from longs placed just below). When price finally breaks that level, it is not "support breaking" — it is smart money reaching down into that pool to fill buy orders against all those sell stops being triggered. Price will often wick below the level and immediately reverse. That wick is the liquidity sweep.

### Buy Side and Sell Side Liquidity

**Buy Side Liquidity (BSL)** sits above swing highs. It consists of:
- Buy stops from short sellers protecting their positions
- Breakout buy orders from retail traders trying to "catch the move"

**Sell Side Liquidity (SSL)** sits below swing lows. It consists of:
- Sell stops from long traders protecting their positions
- Breakdown sell orders from retail traders trying to "catch the move" down

The market oscillates between these two pools. It will sweep one side, reverse, and target the other. This is the fundamental rhythm of all price movement. Once you train your eye to see BSL and SSL, you will never look at a chart the same way again.

**How it shows up on the chart:**

Draw a horizontal line at every obvious swing high and swing low. Those lines are not "support and resistance" — they are targets. The market is going to seek those levels because that is where the orders are. When you see price run above a prior high by a few ticks and then immediately sell off, that is BSL being swept. When price dips below a prior low and immediately rallies, that is SSL being swept.

## Part II: Market Structure — The Skeleton of Price

### Swing Highs and Swing Lows

Before anything else, you must be able to correctly identify swing structure. A **swing high** is a candle high with lower highs on both sides. A **swing low** is a candle low with higher lows on both sides. These are the building blocks of everything.

On a higher timeframe (4H, Daily), these swings represent **pivot points** — the major turning points where institutional order flow shifted direction. On a lower timeframe (15m, 5m), the same principles apply but at a finer resolution, giving you micro-structure within the larger moves.

Do not confuse every minor fluctuation with a swing. On the 4H chart, you are looking for the major pivots — the points where price meaningfully changed direction and created a leg of movement. If you are marking every three-candle bump as a swing, you have lost the forest for the trees.

### Market Structure Shift (MSS) and Change of Character (ChoCh)

**Market Structure** is the sequence of swing highs and swing lows.

- **Bullish structure:** Higher highs and higher lows. Each swing low holds above the prior swing low, and each swing high exceeds the prior swing high.
- **Bearish structure:** Lower highs and lower lows. Each swing high fails below the prior swing high, and each swing low breaks below the prior swing low.

A **Change of Character (ChoCh)** is the first indication that the prevailing structure may be shifting. In a downtrend, a ChoCh occurs when price breaks above a recent swing high — specifically, the first swing high that formed after the most recent significant low. This is the market telling you for the first time that the sellers may have lost control.

A ChoCh is not confirmation. It is a warning shot. It says: "Pay attention — something may be changing." Many traders get burned because they treat a ChoCh as a trade signal. It is not. It is a structural observation.

A **Market Structure Shift (MSS)** is the confirmed break. After a ChoCh, if price continues to build structure in the new direction — forming a higher low after breaking a swing high in a reversal from bearish to bullish — then you have a genuine shift. The old trend is broken and a new one is forming.

**How it shows up on the chart:**

In a downtrend, you will see lower lows and lower highs marching down the chart. At some point, price makes a low, rallies, pulls back, and then the next rally breaks above the swing high that was created during that first rally off the low. That break is the ChoCh. Mark the level where that swing high was — that is your break point. If price then pulls back and holds above the prior low (forming a higher low), you now have confirmation of a structural shift.

The break point itself — the price level of the swing high that got broken — is significant. It often becomes a reference point for future retracements.

### Break of Structure (BOS)

A **Break of Structure** is a continuation signal within an existing trend. In a bullish trend, each time price makes a new higher high, that is a BOS to the upside — the trend is continuing. In a bearish trend, each new lower low is a BOS to the downside.

BOS tells you the trend is intact. It is not a reversal signal — it is a continuation signal. The market is doing exactly what it was already doing.

**How it shows up on the chart:**

In a bullish trend, mark each swing high. When price closes above a swing high, that is a BOS. The trend continues. If you are long, this confirms your bias. If you are waiting for a pullback entry, a BOS tells you the pullback has not happened yet — do not chase.

## Part III: Time and Price — The Institutional Footprint

### Institutional Order Flow and Displacement

**Displacement** is what it looks like when smart money aggressively enters the market. It appears as one or more large-bodied candles that move decisively in one direction with little to no wicks on the displacement side. When you see three or four consecutive bullish candles with full bodies and tiny lower wicks, that is displacement to the upside. Someone large is buying aggressively.

Displacement is important because it tells you that the move is not random — it is driven by institutional order flow. A slow, grinding move with overlapping candles and long wicks is retail noise. A fast, clean move with displacement candles is institutional intent.

**How it shows up on the chart:**

After a liquidity sweep (say price takes out a swing low), watch for the reversal candle and the candles that follow. If the reversal produces large-bodied candles with minimal wicks moving away from the swept level, that is displacement. It confirms that the sweep was purposeful — smart money filled their orders in the liquidity pool and is now moving price in their intended direction.

### Fair Value Gaps (FVG)

A **Fair Value Gap** is a three-candle pattern where the range of the first candle and the range of the third candle do not overlap. This creates a gap in the middle candle's range where price moved so aggressively that it left an inefficiency — an area where not all orders were filled.

For a **bullish FVG**: The high of candle 1 is lower than the low of candle 3. The gap between them (the space from candle 1's high to candle 3's low) is the FVG.

For a **bearish FVG**: The low of candle 1 is higher than the high of candle 3. The gap between them is the FVG.

The market has a tendency to return to these gaps and "fill" them — meaning price will retrace into the gap at some point. This is because the inefficiency represents unfinished business. Not all orders at those prices were matched, so the market revisits the area to complete the auction process.

FVGs are one of the most reliable features on any chart. They act as magnets for price and as zones where you can anticipate reactions. A bullish FVG in the context of a bullish trend is a potential entry zone on a pullback. A bearish FVG in a bearish trend is a potential entry zone on a rally.

**How it shows up on the chart:**

Look at any strong impulsive move — a displacement candle or sequence of them. In the middle of that move, you will often find candles where the prior candle's high does not touch the next candle's low (bullish) or the prior candle's low does not touch the next candle's high (bearish). Draw a rectangle over that gap. That is your FVG. When price retraces to that rectangle in the future, watch for a reaction.

Not all FVGs are created equal. An FVG created during a displacement move following a liquidity sweep on a higher timeframe is far more significant than a random gap in choppy price action.

### Consequent Encroachment (CE)

**Consequent Encroachment** is the 50% level of a Fair Value Gap. Just as price often reacts at the 50% level of a range, it frequently reacts at the midpoint of an FVG. If a bullish FVG spans from 4500 to 4510, the CE is at 4505.

This matters because sometimes price will not fill the entire FVG — it will reach the CE and reverse. Other times, price will trade through the CE, and the full gap will be filled. The CE gives you a more precise reference point within the gap.

**How it shows up on the chart:**

Once you have identified an FVG, mark its midpoint. Watch price as it returns to the gap. If it bounces right at the midpoint and reverses, that is CE acting as a precision level. If it blows through the CE, the FVG may fill entirely or even become invalidated.

## Part IV: Premium and Discount — Where Smart Money Buys and Sells

### Equilibrium, Premium, and Discount

Take any range — from a swing low to a swing high. The 50% level of that range is **equilibrium**. Above equilibrium is **premium**. Below equilibrium is **discount**.

This is not arbitrary. It is based on the institutional concept of fair value. Smart money buys at a discount and sells at a premium. They do not chase breakouts. They do not buy at highs because "momentum." They wait for price to retrace into discount territory and then deploy their orders.

In a bullish trend, you want to be looking for longs when price pulls back into the discount zone (below the 50% level of the most recent swing). In a bearish trend, you want shorts when price rallies into the premium zone (above the 50% level).

**How it shows up on the chart:**

Identify the most recent complete swing — from a significant low to a significant high (or vice versa). Use a Fibonacci retracement tool from the low to the high. The 50% level is equilibrium. Below 50% is discount. Above 50% is premium.

Now look at where price pulls back to before continuing the trend. You will notice that in a healthy bullish trend, pullbacks tend to find support in the discount zone — often at the 62%, 70.5%, or 79% retracement levels. These are the zones where institutional buyers are waiting.

### Optimal Trade Entry (OTE)

The **Optimal Trade Entry** zone is the sweet spot within the discount (for longs) or premium (for shorts) where the highest probability entries occur. This is typically the area between the 62% and 79% Fibonacci retracement of the most recent swing leg.

The OTE is not a magic zone that always works. It is a probabilistic zone that, when combined with other confluence factors (an FVG sitting in that zone, a liquidity sweep just below, displacement confirming the move), gives you the highest quality entries.

**How it shows up on the chart:**

After a BOS in a bullish trend, wait for the pullback. Draw your Fibonacci from the swing low to the swing high that created the BOS. The zone between 62% and 79% is your OTE. If there is a bullish FVG sitting within that OTE zone, and price has just swept a minor low on the way down, you have a textbook entry. You place your long, with a stop below the swing low, and target the next BSL level above.

## Part V: PD Arrays — The Institutional Toolbox

### What Are PD Arrays?

**PD Arrays** (Premium/Discount Arrays) are specific price levels and zones that act as institutional reference points. They are the areas on the chart where smart money is likely to interact with price. Think of them as the footprints that institutional order flow leaves behind.

The key PD Arrays include:

**Bullish PD Arrays** (found in discount, used for long entries):
- Bullish Order Blocks
- Bullish Fair Value Gaps
- Bullish Breaker Blocks
- Sell Side Liquidity (as a sweep target before reversal)

**Bearish PD Arrays** (found in premium, used for short entries):
- Bearish Order Blocks
- Bearish Fair Value Gaps
- Bearish Breaker Blocks
- Buy Side Liquidity (as a sweep target before reversal)

### Order Blocks (OB)

An **Order Block** is the last candle of the opposite direction before a displacement move. In simple terms:

- A **bullish Order Block** is the last down-close candle before a strong move up (displacement to the upside).
- A **bearish Order Block** is the last up-close candle before a strong move down (displacement to the downside).

The theory is straightforward: that last candle before displacement is where institutional orders were being accumulated. Smart money was building their position during that final period of counter-trend movement, and the displacement that followed is the proof of their intent.

When price returns to the Order Block in the future, it is returning to the zone where those institutional orders were originally placed. There is a high probability of a reaction at that level because:

1. The institutions who placed orders there may add to their position
2. The level represents a validated zone of institutional interest
3. Unfilled orders from the original accumulation may still be resting there

**How it shows up on the chart:**

After a liquidity sweep and displacement, scroll back to the last candle before the displacement began. If the displacement was bullish, find the last bearish candle — the last red candle before the green displacement candles took over. The range of that candle (open to close, or more conservatively, high to low) is your bullish Order Block.

Mark it. When price pulls back to that zone, watch for a reaction. The best Order Blocks are the ones that sit within the OTE zone of the larger swing and have an FVG directly above them (for bullish) or below them (for bearish).

### Breaker Blocks

A **Breaker Block** is a failed Order Block that has been broken through, and now acts as a reference on the opposite side. When a bullish Order Block fails — meaning price breaks below it — that zone flips from being a support zone to a resistance zone.

This concept is powerful because it captures the idea of trapped traders. Everyone who bought at the bullish Order Block is now underwater. Their positions are showing losses, and many of them will be looking to exit at breakeven if price returns to that zone. That selling pressure at the old OB creates resistance — the zone has "broken" and now serves the opposite purpose.

**How it shows up on the chart:**

Find an Order Block that was created and then subsequently broken through (price traded through it in the opposite direction). The range of that broken OB is now a Breaker Block. In a bearish scenario, a broken bullish OB becomes a bearish Breaker — when price rallies back up to that zone, expect selling pressure. You will often see price wick into the Breaker and reject.

### Mitigation Blocks

A **Mitigation Block** is related to Breaker Blocks but represents a zone where smart money returns to offset (mitigate) losing positions from a previous trade. If institutions bought in a zone that subsequently traded lower, they are holding unrealized losses. When price eventually returns to that zone, they will often sell to mitigate those losses — closing out the losing position at breakeven or a small loss.

This is a more advanced concept, but the key takeaway is: zones where institutional orders were placed and then price moved adversely will act as reaction zones when price revisits them. The direction of the reaction depends on what the institutions need to do — if they are mitigating longs, they will sell when price returns.

## Part VI: Time — The Overlooked Dimension

### Killzones

Institutional order flow is not random across the trading day. It is concentrated in specific windows when the major financial centers are active and overlapping. These windows are called **Killzones**.

**London Killzone:** 2:00 AM – 5:00 AM New York time
This is when the London session opens and European institutional order flow enters the market. It often sets the high or low of the day, particularly on days when the daily bias is clear.

**New York Killzone:** 7:00 AM – 10:00 AM New York time
This is the most important killzone for US-centric instruments (ES, NQ, US equities). The overlap with London creates massive liquidity, and this is when the most significant moves of the day tend to occur.

**London Close Killzone:** 10:00 AM – 12:00 PM New York time
As London traders close positions, there is often a reversal or consolidation of the move that occurred during the NY open. This killzone is useful for taking profits or for counter-trend setups.

**Asian Killzone:** 7:00 PM – 10:00 PM New York time
The Asian session typically creates a range that London and NY will later sweep for liquidity. The Asian range high and low are key reference points for the following sessions.

**How it shows up on the chart:**

Mark the killzone times on your chart with vertical lines or session boxes. Now observe where the day's significant moves originate. You will see that the majority of trending moves begin within a killzone — particularly the London and NY killzones. Moves that occur outside killzones (the "dead zones" between sessions) are typically low-quality and prone to reversal.

If you want to trade, trade within killzones. If you are outside a killzone, you are watching, not executing.

### Power of Three (AMD)

Every significant move in the market follows a three-phase pattern: **Accumulation, Manipulation, Distribution** (AMD).

**Accumulation** is the quiet phase. Price is consolidating in a range, building up orders on both sides. This often occurs during the Asian session or the early hours before a killzone.

**Manipulation** is the fake move. Price breaks out of the accumulation range in one direction — sweeping liquidity — only to reverse. This is the stop hunt. The manipulation phase tricks retail traders into the wrong side of the market and provides smart money with the liquidity they need.

**Distribution** is the real move. After the manipulation phase sweeps liquidity, price reverses and moves aggressively in the intended direction. This is where displacement occurs, FVGs are created, and the trend leg plays out.

**How it shows up on the chart:**

Look at any trending day. Before the killzone, price was in a range (accumulation). Early in the killzone, price broke one side of that range — sweeping the high or low (manipulation). Then price reversed and moved strongly in the opposite direction for the rest of the session (distribution).

This is the daily AMD cycle, but it fractalizes. You can see it on a weekly basis (Monday accumulation, Tuesday manipulation, Wednesday–Thursday distribution). You can see it on a 15-minute chart within a single killzone. The pattern is the same at every scale because the underlying mechanics — liquidity sweep followed by displacement — are the same.

### ICT Weekly Profiles

The days of the week have tendencies:

- **Monday:** Often a continuation of Friday or a range-building day. It sets the week's initial range. Smart money observes retail positioning.
- **Tuesday:** Frequently the manipulation day. Tuesday often creates the week's high or low — the "Judas swing" that traps traders before the real move.
- **Wednesday:** The most common day for the week's real move to begin. After Tuesday's manipulation, Wednesday's move often establishes the dominant direction.
- **Thursday:** Continuation of Wednesday's move or the beginning of profit-taking. Often reaches the week's directional target.
- **Friday:** Profit-taking and position squaring before the weekend. Be cautious — Friday moves can be deceptive as they are driven by position management rather than new directional intent.

This does not mean every week follows this script. But over hundreds of weeks, you will see these tendencies emerge. When your higher timeframe analysis aligns with the weekly profile — say you are bearish and it is Tuesday with a run on BSL — that confluence adds weight to your thesis.

## Part VII: Higher Timeframe Analysis — The Compass

### The Importance of Top-Down Analysis

You do not start your analysis on the 5-minute chart. You start on the Monthly, work down to the Weekly, then the Daily, then the 4H, and only then do you look at the 15m or 5m for execution.

The higher timeframe tells you **what** should happen. The lower timeframe tells you **when** and **where** to execute.

If the Daily chart is in a bullish trend and price has just swept a Daily SSL and is sitting in a Daily bullish OB, your bias is long. You are not looking for shorts. Period. It does not matter what the 5-minute chart looks like — the higher timeframe context dictates your directional bias.

### Previous Highs and Lows (PDH/PDL, PWH/PWL, PMH/PML)

**Previous Day High/Low (PDH/PDL):** The high and low of the prior trading day. These are the most immediate liquidity targets. Sweeping the PDH or PDL is a daily ritual in the market.

**Previous Week High/Low (PWH/PWL):** The high and low of the prior trading week. These are significant liquidity pools and often act as the primary targets for the current week's moves. For futures, the week begins Sunday at 6:00 PM Eastern Time.

**Previous Month High/Low (PMH/PML):** Monthly levels are the big picture targets. When the market sweeps a PMH or PML, it is a significant event that often coincides with major reversals or continuations.

**How it shows up on the chart:**

Mark these levels every day, every week, every month. They are your roadmap. When you ask, "Where is price going?" — the answer is almost always one of these levels. Price is either heading toward PDH, PDL, PWH, PWL, PMH, or PML. It is either going to sweep one of them or react at one of them.

The PW50% (50% of the previous week's range) and PD50% (50% of the previous day's range) are also significant as equilibrium levels.

## Part VIII: Putting It All Together — A Framework for Analysis

### The Daily Preparation Ritual

1. **Mark your levels:** PDH, PDL, PWH, PWL, PMH, PML. These are your targets and reference points.
2. **Identify higher timeframe bias:** What is the Daily doing? What is the 4H doing? Where is price in relation to the higher timeframe structure? Is price in premium or discount of the larger swing?
3. **Identify the nearest liquidity pools:** Where are the obvious highs and lows that price is likely to target? Which side has more liquidity — BSL or SSL?
4. **Mark your PD Arrays:** Where are the FVGs, OBs, and Breaker Blocks on the 4H and 1H that align with your bias? These are your potential entry zones.
5. **Wait for the killzone:** Do not force trades before the killzone. Let the market come to you.
6. **Watch for the manipulation:** At the start of the killzone, watch for the liquidity sweep — the fake move that traps retail.
7. **Enter on the displacement:** After the sweep, wait for displacement in the real direction. Enter at the FVG or OB that forms, with your stop beyond the liquidity sweep.
8. **Target the opposing liquidity:** If you went long after an SSL sweep, your target is the nearest BSL — a previous high that holds buy side liquidity.

### The Hierarchy of Confluence

Not all setups are equal. The best trades have multiple elements aligning:

1. **Higher timeframe bias** — Daily or 4H structure supports the direction
2. **Liquidity sweep** — Price has just swept an obvious high or low
3. **Displacement** — The move off the sweep is aggressive, with large-bodied candles
4. **PD Array alignment** — An FVG or OB sits in the OTE zone of the move
5. **Time** — The setup occurs within a killzone
6. **Weekly profile** — The day of the week aligns (e.g., Tuesday sweep, Wednesday move)

A setup with all six elements is rare but extremely high probability. A setup with three or four is tradeable. A setup with only one or two is a gamble — stay out.

## Part IX: What This Framework Is Not

This is not a mechanical system. You cannot code these concepts into a bot and walk away. The market is a living, adaptive mechanism driven by human decision-making at the institutional level. The concepts here give you a framework for understanding what is happening and why, but applying them requires discretion, experience, and patience.

You will not catch every move. You will miss setups. You will sometimes be wrong about the bias. That is the cost of trading. What this framework gives you is the ability to be wrong in small amounts and right in large amounts — because you are trading from zones of institutional significance with defined risk, rather than gambling on indicator signals that have no connection to how price actually moves.

Study the charts. Mark up historical price action. Go through every day of the past year on your instrument and identify the AMD cycle, the liquidity sweeps, the FVGs, the OBs. Do this until you can see the patterns without marking anything. That is when this framework becomes second nature — and that is when you become dangerous.

The charts never lie. Your job is to learn to read what they are actually saying.
