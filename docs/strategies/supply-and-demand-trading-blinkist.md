# Supply and Demand Trading: How To Master The Trading Zones

**By Frank Miller** · Blinkist-style summary · ~15 min read

---

## What's it about?

*Supply and Demand Trading* strips price action down to one simple idea: markets move because big institutions place orders too large to fill in one go. The unfilled portion sits at specific price levels, waiting. When price returns to those levels, the leftover orders fire — and price reverses sharply.

Frank Miller turns this institutional footprint into a repeatable trading system. You learn to spot the zones where smart money parked orders, judge which zones are still "loaded," and trade reversals from them with mechanical entries, stops, and targets. No indicators. No prediction. Just zones, trend, and the curve.

## Who's it for?

- Discretionary traders tired of indicator soup who want a price-action-only framework
- Swing and intraday traders looking for high-RR setups (3:1+ is the floor)
- Anyone who's heard of "order blocks" or "institutional levels" and wants the underlying logic
- Traders who already know structure (HH/HL/LH/LL) and want to graduate to zone-based entries

## Blink 1 — Price moves because of imbalance, not magic

Every chart move is a battle between buyers and sellers. When one side massively outsizes the other, price doesn't drift — it gaps, spikes, or runs in a near-vertical leg. That violence is the fingerprint of an institution unloading orders the market can't absorb.

The key insight: institutions can't fill a 10,000-contract order at one price without crashing the market against themselves. So they fill what they can, the price runs, and the rest of the order sits unfilled at the origin. That origin is a **zone**. When price returns there, the unfilled portion executes — and price reverses again.

Retail traders see "support" and "resistance" as lines. Miller wants you to see them as zones with stored orders, with a shelf life, and with a quality grade.

## Blink 2 — Zones come in four flavors

A zone is always a small consolidation (the **base**) that precedes a sharp move. The shape of the moves around the base names the pattern:

- **DBR — Drop-Base-Rally** → demand zone (buyers absorbed the drop, then exploded up)
- **RBR — Rally-Base-Rally** → demand zone in an uptrend (continuation)
- **RBD — Rally-Base-Drop** → supply zone (sellers absorbed the rally, then crashed)
- **DBD — Drop-Base-Drop** → supply zone in a downtrend (continuation)

The reversal patterns (DBR, RBD) mark turning points. The continuation patterns (RBR, DBD) mark pullbacks within an existing trend. Both are tradeable, but they tell different stories about who's in control.

## Blink 3 — A zone's quality is everything

Not all zones are equal. Before you trade one, grade it:

1. **Strength of departure** — did price leave the base with violence (long-bodied candles, gaps, no overlap)? Weak departure = weak zone.
2. **Freshness** — has price returned to this zone yet? A *fresh* (untested) zone has the full institutional order still waiting. A second visit has eaten into it. Third visit, you skip.
3. **Time at the base** — short bases (1–6 candles) are best. The longer price loiters, the more orders get filled, and the less is left for your entry.
4. **Curve location** — a demand zone near the bottom of the larger range is gold; one near the top is a trap.

A **fresh, sharp-departure, short-base, well-located** zone is the A+ setup. Take those. Pass the rest.

## Blink 4 — The Curve: peak, equilibrium, valley

Zoom out one timeframe higher than your trading TF and ask: where am I on the larger curve?

- **Valley** (bottom of higher-TF range) → only buy demand zones, never sell supply
- **Peak** (top of higher-TF range) → only sell supply zones, never buy demand
- **Equilibrium** (middle) → no man's land; wait for price to pick a side

This single filter eliminates most losing trades. Selling supply at the valley is fighting the larger demand imbalance below you. Buying demand at the peak is fighting the larger supply above. The curve tells you which direction the higher-TF institutions are leaning — trade with them.

## Blink 5 — Trend is your second filter

Inside the curve location, identify the trend on your trading TF using market structure:

- **Uptrend** = higher highs + higher lows. Buy fresh demand zones on pullbacks (RBR).
- **Downtrend** = lower highs + lower lows. Sell fresh supply zones on bounces (DBD).
- **Range** = no clear HH/HL or LH/LL. Trade reversals (DBR at range low, RBD at range high).

Trend + curve location must agree. If the higher-TF curve says "valley" but your trading TF prints lower highs and lower lows, you're early — wait for structure to flip before buying.

## Blink 6 — Entry, stop, target — mechanical, not emotional

Once you've found an A+ zone aligned with curve and trend:

- **Entry**: limit order at the *proximal* line of the zone (the edge price hits first). No chasing, no confirming, no "waiting for a wick." The order sits and either fills or doesn't.
- **Stop loss**: just beyond the *distal* line (the far edge of the zone) plus a small buffer for spread/noise. If price closes through the distal, the institution's order is done — you're wrong.
- **Target**: the next opposing zone, or 3R minimum. Below 3:1 reward-to-risk, the setup isn't worth taking.

Set it, walk away. Discretionary management is where most traders give back the edge.

## Blink 7 — The mitigated-zone trap

A zone is "mitigated" once price returns and tags it. After the first tag, the institutional order is partly or fully filled. The level may still react — but with diminishing strength.

Miller's rule: trade only **fresh** (unmitigated) zones for new entries. Use mitigated zones only as confluence, exit targets, or invalidation references. Watching a twice-tested zone hoping for a third bounce is how retail accounts die slowly.

A subtle corollary: a zone that's been *wick-tested* (price tagged it but didn't close inside) is still mostly fresh. A zone that's been *body-closed* through is dead. Read the candle close, not just the high or low.

## Blink 8 — Multi-timeframe alignment is the edge

The fractal nature of price means zones nest inside zones. A 15-minute demand zone often sits inside a 4-hour demand zone, which sits inside a daily demand zone. When all three line up, the setup is institutional-grade.

The workflow:

1. **Daily / weekly**: identify the curve — are we at peak, equilibrium, or valley?
2. **4-hour**: find the dominant trend and the major fresh zones
3. **15-min / 1-hour**: refine entry inside a higher-TF zone using a smaller fresh zone

The smaller zone gives you a tighter stop. The larger zone gives you the bigger target. RR balloons.

## Blink 9 — Risk management is the strategy

Even A+ setups lose. Miller's framework assumes a 50–60% win rate at 3:1 RR — which is wildly profitable, but only if position sizing survives the inevitable losing streak.

Rules of thumb from the book:

- Risk 1% of account per trade, max. Halve it after two consecutive losses.
- Cap simultaneous open trades at 3–5 to avoid correlated blowups (especially in FX, where pairs share legs).
- Track every trade in a journal: zone grade, curve location, trend, RR, outcome. Patterns in your *own* errors emerge within 30–50 trades.

The edge isn't the zone. The edge is taking only A+ zones, sized small, repeatedly, for years.

## Blink 10 — Psychology: the zone trades you back

The hardest part isn't finding zones — it's letting limit orders fill into falling/rising price without intervening. Your gut will scream "this one's different, it'll blow through." Sometimes it does. Most times it doesn't. Either way, the system only works if you trade every A+ setup.

Miller's antidote: detach from individual outcomes. Track results in 50-trade blocks, not trade-by-trade. Win rate, average RR, and expectancy are the only numbers that matter. P&L on any single day is noise.

If you can't take a 4R loser without flinching, you can't take the 6R winners that pay for it. Same emotional muscle.

## Final summary

Markets are driven by institutional imbalance. That imbalance leaves footprints — small consolidations followed by violent departures. Those footprints are zones, and they store unfilled orders that fire when price returns.

The system is brutally simple:

1. Find a fresh zone with a sharp departure, short base, and good location on the higher-TF curve.
2. Confirm trend on your trading TF agrees with curve direction.
3. Place a limit at the proximal, stop beyond the distal, target ≥ 3R or the next opposing zone.
4. Risk 1%, size mechanically, journal every trade.
5. Take A+ setups only. Pass everything else.

The book's quiet promise: most retail traders fail because they trade too many setups, too large, with no framework. Trade fewer setups, smaller, with this framework, and the math works.

## Key takeaways

- **Zones over lines** — support and resistance are areas with stored institutional orders, not pixel-thin lines
- **Four patterns** — DBR, RBR, RBD, DBD; reversal vs. continuation
- **Quality grading** — fresh + sharp departure + short base + good curve location = A+
- **Curve filter** — trade demand at valleys, supply at peaks, never the inverse
- **Mechanical execution** — limit at proximal, stop beyond distal, target ≥ 3R
- **Fresh only** — mitigated zones are confluence, not entries
- **Multi-TF alignment** — small zone inside bigger zone = best RR
- **Risk first** — 1% per trade, journal everything, results in 50-trade blocks
